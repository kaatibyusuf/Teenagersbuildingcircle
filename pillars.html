Four pillars. One teenager, built whole.

Every student in the Circle moves through all four at once, deen, educational development, skills, and personal development, each placed individually by the diagnostic assessment rather than by age. Here is exactly what each pillar covers, how it's taught, and what a term inside it actually looks like.

Pillar One

Deen

Deen is the foundation everything else in the Circle is built on. Students study aqeedah, the fiqh of worship appropriate to their age, seerah, adab, and work on their Qur'an through memorisation and tajweed. Every ruling and every piece of aqeedah is taught with its evidence, so a student understands why they believe and practise what they do, not only that they must.

Fiqh matters are taught according to the Maliki madhab, and where scholars differ, that difference is shown honestly rather than presented as settled.

  • Aqeedah grounded in evidence, not rote statements
  • Fiqh of salah, purification, fasting, and zakah
  • Seerah studied for its lessons, not just its events
  • Qur'an memorisation and tajweed, tracked individually

Pillar Two

Educational Development

This pillar strengthens the academic foundation every teenager needs, regardless of what they go on to study. Sessions cover reading comprehension, writing, study skills, numeracy, and critical thinking. A student who is already strong is pushed into research and independent study rather than left to coast; a student who is behind is brought up steadily rather than rushed.

Placement here comes directly from the diagnostic assessment, so no student sits through material far above or far below where they actually are.

  • Reading comprehension and vocabulary building
  • Structured writing, from paragraphs to full essays
  • Numeracy from basic fluency through introductory algebra
  • Study skills, exam technique, and independent research

Pillar Three

Skills Acquisition

This pillar builds practical, transferable ability: writing and communication, basic digital literacy, an introduction to entrepreneurial and financial thinking, and structured ways of solving problems. Every session favours output over theory, a lesson on writing ends with the student having written something, not merely having heard about writing.

The intent is that a student leaves the Circle able to produce something of real value, not only able to recite what they were taught.

  • Professional writing and communication
  • Digital literacy and online safety
  • Budgeting and basic financial thinking
  • Structured problem-solving frameworks

Pillar Four

Personal Development

This pillar shapes character directly: self-discipline, emotional regulation, goal-setting, and confidence in speech and leadership. Three habits sit at its centre and are reinforced every single week rather than taught once and left behind: reading, time management, and internet decorum.

A student is taught to plan their week around their salah rather than fitting salah around everything else, to build an appetite for reading rather than treating it as an obligation, and to carry themselves well in digital spaces most adults were never taught to navigate either.

  • A rotating reading list with personal reflection each week
  • Weekly time-management planning, reviewed with a facilitator
  • Islamic etiquette applied specifically to online conduct
  • Confidence, goal-setting, and emotional self-regulation

"They will remember whether you showed up, whether you noticed them, and whether you meant what you taught." From the Facilitator's Handbook

Behind Every Pillar

Taught by a facilitator who knows your child

Each pillar is led by a facilitator selected for competence in that subject and for the character to mentor a teenager honestly, not by a recorded course.

Placement first, always. Before a student sits in any pillar's first live session, the diagnostic assessment has already told their facilitator exactly where to begin, and where each of the other three pillars stands too. A student can be Advanced in one pillar and Foundational in another, and the Circle is built to hold both at once.
